Case study: My own website

In 2016, I launched a one-page website that had many problems. To name a few, it had poor traffic and almost none returning visitors. The last straw was that eight out of ten people left it within a few seconds. I decided to redesign the whole thing. Although to do a fair job, I needed to identify all the problems I could. I thought UX design could help... And it did!

The purpose of this page is to register the path that took me from a flawed website to a user-centred one. Below is the summed content of my process, based on research, strategy and design.

Should you need more details, please click the buttons to unfold each step. Plus, the text links will take you to the source of every related bit of information.

Research

Somehow, I knew that my website had problems. At first, I thought I was aware of everything. But, after a week of researching, I learned that I didn't know the half of it. Yikes!

An analysis of data gave me some facts: poor traffic, low engagement and an enormous bounce rate. I had a few explanations for all this. Although, I didn't learn the actual reasons until I performed a usability test.

This test consisted of five people using my website by my side. That allowed me to see the actual issues or pain points. A hidden portfolio made it useless. A non-standard menu frustrated the testers. They agreed on the fact that I should clarify my services and availability. Man, I had no idea how confusing my website was.

My son, helping me identify the pain points. It may seem trivial, but it was him who made me first realize that two buttons for the three languages were a problem.

Quantitative user data

From January to May 2018, I followed the performance of my website in Google Analytics. I centred my analysis on the audience, acquisition and behaviour. These were the main issues observed:

  • Low traffic and lack of engagement. There were 83 sessions completed by 58 people. Only nine of them returned during those five months.
  • Huge bounce rate. 83% of the users left after visiting a single page. The average session lasted 46 seconds.
  • Weak referencing. Most of the people who visited the website arrived by tapping its URL address. Only nine came from social media and seven by organic search.

Why was that? Some hypotheses I had

  • Misuse of a resource and lack of originality. I used a template. Although it was a nice one, it didn't expose my abilities as a web designer. The icing on the cake was that I forced it into showing a whole case study within a popup window.
  • Need for focus. My goal was to attract clients as a graphic designer, photographer and illustrator. That was too many things to offer at once. I should have narrowed my services.
  • Impossible to measure or share. As the portfolio items opened in a popup window, there was no way to register the traffic towards a project. I couldn't measure which content was more interesting. Nobody was able to share a specific work with anyone.
  • Dated content. Since I released it, I forgot about it. I didn't take the time to keep the portfolio up to date.

You'd think that at this moment I've had all the reasons why my website was failing. But, to my surprise, I haven't seen anything yet. Not until I watched real people struggling with it.

My old website. When users clicked on an item from the portfolio, it opened as a popup window.

Qualitative user data

Within a week, I scheduled a usability test with five co-workers. 45 minutes at lunchtime, one at a time. Their profiles didn't match my target audience as they should. Still, observing them while exploring the website opened my eyes in an unforeseen way.

My first experience map. A weird mix of Spanglish and Franpagnol.

I registered all my notes in an experience map. For this, I followed Chris Nodder's instructions. It was a tangle of languages, mixing English, French and Spanish, but it worked. It allowed me to visualize at once the observations I gathered during the usability test.

As I was working alone, I craved for a team, so I looked for help. My husband and our son assisted me through the process. Even if they didn't observe the users, the notes came in handy. Together, we identified the hardest tasks for the users: the pain points.

The paint points

These are the main issues revealed by the testers:

  • The portfolio items looked flat. A few users ignored that the thumbnails were clickable and so they didn't explore them. To me, this is the most severe problem. An invisible portfolio makes the whole website of a creative person meaningless.
  • Non-standard menu labels. Phrases like "What I do" instead of "Portfolio" puzzled the users.
  • Purposelessness. All testers criticized the lacking of an explicit call-to-action.
  • Content set under the fold. On the main page, a large photograph pushed the information downwards and out of the screen. On the portfolio pieces, a couple of people ignored that there was more content below.
  • Weak flow. Some users had troubles finding their way back to the home page from the portfolio windows. It was also hard to differentiate the visited sections from the ones they haven't seen yet.

Strategy

Once I finished the research, I began to set my goals. I outlined them in two directions: for the users and the business. The first ones aimed at the improvement of the user's experience. The other ones, to secure my success as an entrepreneur.

Next, I decided to narrow my practice and choose an audience. As this is my first exposure to UX design, I needed to simplify all I could to learn faster and stay motivated.

My audience would be creative men and women, like photographers, illustrators and artists. People whose websites would revolve around a portfolio. I could work for them as a part of a team or as a consultant. So, I defined the profile of two primary personas: a potential boss and a client. They would keep me focused along the way. Also, I will measure these goals three months after the launching.

Thanks to these steps I underwent the best creative process I've had as a professional. I only had to look for solutions to the identified problems, always keeping my personas in mind. Then, I would picture them interacting with those solutions.

From left to right: sketches, scenarios, storyboards and paper prototype.

Establishing goals for the user and the business

The pain points revealed in the research period gave me the goals for my website's redesign. Inspired by Jake Knapp, I turned them into how may we questions. I found this approach very enlightening. Each idea, every single sketch would answer one or some of the following questions:

  • How may we make the portfolio items seem clickable?
  • How may we display easy-to-understand menu labels?
  • How may we deliver a website with a clear mission?
  • How may we reveal the whole content in the portfolio section?
  • How may we provide a seamless browsing experience?

So far, so good, right? Not quite. While these questionings acknowledged the pain points, they neglected my own goals. And this is also important for design thinking. After all, as Jeff Gothelf says, it offers solutions for the client that also benefit the business.

My goals as an entrepreneur arose from the three main issues I observed in Google Analytics. I will check them within three months after the launching of the new website:

  • Double the traffic and increase the engagement by 40%. At least 116 people should come to the new website, of which 15 or more would be returning users.
  • Reduce the bounce rate to 60% or less. No more than six out of 10 people should leave the site without visiting other pages. If the average session lasts more than one minute, I'll throw a party!
  • Improve the referencing. Most of the traffic should come from social media or external links.

By this moment, I was well aware of the problems I had to solve and why. Next, I needed to know for whom —besides myself— I was going to do it all.

Defining the target audience (Personas)

When I decided to narrow my practice, everything started to make sense. I knew that I wanted to serve creative people and this simplified my learning journey on UX. I then chose to address my work towards two primary personas:

  • A potential boss, who would push me to develop my process and explain it.
  • A potential client, who would drive me to understand my audience and their needs.

Then, a secondary persona came to mind:

  • A student, who would remind me to keep my message clear, relevant and simple.

Élise, the tech-savvy hirer

“As a director of Marketing, I want to hire a UX designer so that my team can develop user-centred websites.” —Élise

Born in Quebec City, 43-years-old, married, two kids. She's the director of the Marketing department at a public institution in Montreal. She's bilingual by nature, as she learned French from her mother and English from her father. She's a very logical person and has a strong character.

Extrovert. Skilled with technology. Élise has a master degree in Marketing and business management. As a boss, she's maternalist and likes to control everything. But she trusts her employees and knows how to take advantage of their strengths. Élise is a workaholic, stubborn and perfectionist woman.

Her goals are:

  • To hire a UX designer for her team.
  • To produce the websites of artisans and artists represented by the institution.

Normand, the artsy illustrator

“As an illustrator, I want to hire a web designer so that I can promote and sell my work online.” —Normand

Born in France, he's been living in Montreal for 27 years. 62 years old. Separated, no kids. Nowadays, Normand lives with his girlfriend.

He works for a reputable agency based in Montreal and France. He only creates through manual techniques. Old school, back in France he studied arts. Normand doesn't feel at ease with technology. Though, he's able to digitalize his work and send it to the agency by email.

He is very prolific and dedicated to his work. He also participates in solo exhibits with reasonable success. He's calm, wise and organized. Doesn't like to speak or read in English.

His goals are:

  • To hire a freelancer to design his web portfolio.
  • To set up an online shop that allows him to sell his work.

Noelia, the student

“As a graphic design student, I want to learn about UX design so that I can specialize myself in that area.” —Noelia

Born and raised in Colombia, she's 21 years old and single. No kids. As she is concluding a diploma in graphic design, she's considering a master in UX design. Noelia is very curious and autodidact. She's also proficient with technology.

She's an introvert. Her mother language is Spanish, but she's able to communicate in English.

Her goals are:

  • To find resources about UX design.
  • To better understand the UX design process.

Generating ideas (Ideation)

Generating ideas while commuting by train. Here, I was looking at a general view of the interface (desktop and mobile) and a possible sitemap.

Before I started a creative process based on UX design, I sketched for the web without purpose. I put ideas on paper almost without any sense or meaning. In fact, instead of generating lots of ideas, I iterated the same though once and again.

Sometimes, I even stopped after the first idea. In this case, it was the one below. I wanted to create a whole website by using a single colour. It would be pink for English, blue for French and green for Spanish. The main menu would descend while the user scrolls down.

My first idea for this project was a monochromatic website.

I drafted this idea before starting the usability test. By then, I knew that I wanted a standard menu. Although, I still didn't know how hard having two buttons for three languages was for the users.

My process took a big leap forward when I translated the pain points into how may we questions. Up until then, my previous thoughts were often about a general view of the future interface. Later on, I started to devote a whole ideation session to one issue in particular. It was easier to tackle one problem at a time. I felt much more inventive and focused.

An example of general ideas for the homepage (desktop and mobile) and a project page.

An example of ideas generated from one of my questions. Here, I was studying alternatives to make the portfolio items seem clickable.

For several days, I drew different ideas until I got satisfying answers to all the pain points. It all boiled down to two alternatives. One with the main menu on top and the other one, to the left. I then consulted my husband, who is an experienced web developer. We agreed that the most efficient one would be the latter. In my case, it allowed having more content without making it all look busy.

Planning the user's journey (Scenarios and storyboards)

A glimpse into one of my scenarios (back) and storyboards (front).

After the ideation phase, I was too eager to build my prototype. I wanted to skip the making of scenarios and storyboards. But I'm glad I didn't. They helped to visualize Élise, Normand and Noelia interacting with my website. This revealed stuff I wouldn't have considered otherwise.

The imaginary journeys of my personas

Élise stumbled upon the About page through a Google search on a desktop computer. She was looking for a UX designer located in Montreal. First, she scanned the content relevant to her and downloaded my resume. Then, she moved onto the project pages. Finally, she had the human resources team to contact me by phone with the information provided on my cv.

Normand arrived at the homepage by typing my URL address on a tablet. One of his friends had recommended me to him as he wanted to redesign his website. He clicked on the featured project and scanned the process. Normand found the content related to his interest. Therefore, he decided to email me.

Noelia came to a project in progress through social media. She was using her cell phone. She scanned the content and followed some external links. Noelia found this useful and resolved to follow me on Twitter. In this way, she'd keep track of my website to reach other relevant resources.

These hypothetical visits drove me to consider several factors throughout the design phase:

  • The user's profile: sex, age, education, goals and interests.
  • The device: desktop or mobile.
  • The source of traffic: direct, organic search or referenced by other sites.
  • The flow: from the initial page to the desired action.

Another storyboard I made for Élise.

Design

By the time I got to build the paper prototype, I was overexcited. I did make one for the home page. It was pretty basic, but it helped me to play loosely with all the elements of the interface. Next, I needed to see it for real. So, I started drafting a wireframe, then a simple mock-up and before I knew it, I started coding it.

Because my website's structure is rather conventional, I skipped the prototype's usability test. Instead, I decided to follow standard web design practices and validate it once finished.

Since I'm not planning to keep a blog and as I'm learning to code, my website is quite simple. There's no CMS for now.

The rudimentary stages of the user interface (UI)

At this point, I couldn't wait to jump into the design of the user interface. I made a paper prototype and, despite its simplicity, it helped me to play loosely with all its elements.

The paper prototype for my homepage (in progress). I placed a sticky note for each piece of content, so I could move it as needed.

As Chris Nodder advises, I stuck to a standard design and left the creativity for the content. Still, for this same reason and because my website is rather simple, I skipped the usability test he suggests for a paper prototype.

“A good usability professional must be able to identify high-priority problems and make appropriate recommendations—and the best evaluators do this quickly and reliably—but a good designer must also be able to design well in the first place.” —Robert Hoekman Jr.

I wireframed a sort of one-page concept for the homepage. It would contain three parts: intro, portfolio and contact. The first one would take to the about section. The second would showcase three items, and each of them would lead to an extra page.

The wireframe for my homepage. It kept the one-pager notion plus an extra page per case study and the about section.

My original sitemap

Next, I felt ready to draft a simple mockup. I wanted to create an elegant and uncomplicated concept:

  • Because of the lack of contrast, I had to let go of the idea of a single colour for the entire website. I may try it again someday.
  • Instead, I conceived a layout with web safe fonts and the default colours for texts and links. I was trying to build a refined composition with essential tools.
  • I also strived to design with plenty of white space. It helps in delivering a more pleasant look.
  • Finally, I added an explicit link to the About page. After another review of the scenarios and storyboards, I felt the need for it.

A simple mockup drafted in Illustrator. Inspired by Jeremiah Shoaf, I paired Verdana and Georgia.

Once I got to this point, I decided to follow Matej Latin's advice:

“The key is to review the typeface in context. This means with the actual content and on a website. Website, in this case, meaning actual HTML and CSS in a browser, not an approximation in a graphic design tool.” —Matej Latin

I wanted to check in the browsers if this pairing of Verdana and Georgia could work out for my content. And so, I leaped into coding, ignoring that I'd end up building a whole responsive website from scratch. Which, at that time, was one of my wildest dreams. Even after having taken some valid courses like this one. I kid you not.

Choosing and pairing typefaces

Since I started coding, I understood what Matej Latin meant. A design tool can't always show how your content will render on browsers. Once I saw my texts with that pairing of Verdana and Georgia, I thought it all looked plain.

I do like Georgia, though. It's such a beautiful typeface. I know that it'll work fine, even with my longer texts, because it's pretty easy to read on screen.

Impact on the titles and Georgia on the paragraphs.

Instead of pure black, I used a dark grey for all content. The active links were blue and the visited ones, purple. I aimed to make a layout work out of the default settings.

The problem then was in the titles. I tried Impact, but later I found the contrast too overwhelming. Since I still wanted to stick to web safe fonts, I tested Georgia for the titles as well.

The use of colour was bothering me, so I shifted to a white and dark-grey layout.

Georgia bold on the titles and regular on the paragraphs.

Although I liked the result, I desired a mightier contrast. I found it with Raleway. It's not a web safe font, but as I'm only calling it as regular and bold from Google Fonts, the size of my website will stay low.

In short, I'll use two typefaces: a sans-serif for the titles and a serif for the paragraphs.

Raleway on the titles and Georgia on the paragraphs.

Setting a responsive modular scale

Nerdy as I am, I wanted to establish a modular scale for my responsive website. I tried various approaches that I didn't get until I found Zell Liew's proposal. Based on this tool, I chose a different scale ratio for each breakpoint. The smaller the screen, the closest the ratio:

  • Phone — 15:16
  • Tablet — 8:9
  • Desktop — 5:6
  • Big screen — 4:6
“This method works because a smaller ratio produces a scale with smaller differences between font-sizes at each step. It gives us more sizes that are acceptable on a mobile device. ” —Zell Liew

What I like the most about this strategy is that it considers the way we perceive texts in context. When we read on a small screen, there isn't much we can do besides scrolling up and down. That means that titles don't need to be much larger than paragraphs to grab our attention. On big screens though, there are plenty of things that distract us from the content, so titles need to stand out.

A large-scale ratio (1.414) for a desktop computer. It all looked disproportionated.

A small-scale ratio worked better for the different screen sizes.

Setting the menu and colour scheme

When I was happy with my typefaces, I started developing the menus and colour scheme. At first, I separated the languages from the main navigation menu. Then, I added a few images and a simple greyscale layout.

The main menu was to the left while the languages were on a top bar.

Next, I created a colour scheme out of my corporate tone (#fa7057). By following Laura Elizabeth's tutorial, I chose these shades:

#fa7057 — Main accent
#fcb7ab — Secondary accent
#454241 — Darkest shade
#faf3f2 — Lightest shade
White

The darkest shade is for the texts; the accents, for links and buttons. The softer tones are for the backgrounds.

Finally, I joined both menus and added the social media icons. All by keeping them in three differentiated areas, so I could:

  • Reinforce their different functions. People perceive objects as a group when they are near to each other — Law of proximity.
  • Reduce the options that could overwhelm the users. By using an ivy leaf or icons instead of text, users would distinguish these sections as groups — Law of common region.
  • Make the menu easier to digest. Without the separation, it'd had ten items, which is more than what people can keep in their working memory — Miller's law.

The completed menu with the colour scheme applied.

Main principles of user interaction design (UXD) I followed

Navigation

  • Explicit current page. In the main menu, a bolded label indicates the active page.
  • Change of appearance on hover. On a desktop computer, buttons and hyperlinks react when the user hovers.
  • Obvious visited links. Buttons and hyperlinks change colour, so users know they already saw the related page.
  • Accessible homepage. The logo on the header always takes to the main page, regardless of the device.
  • Standard architecture. There are three levels and seven pages. Those in the second level are accessible from the homepage. The ones in the last level (case-studies) are available on the portfolio.
  • Conventional menu. Labelling is standard: home, about, portfolio and contact.
“Users spend most of their time on other sites. This means that users prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know.” —Jakob Nielsen

Accessibility

  • Responsive. Mobile first, the site adapts to four different sizes: phone, tablet, desktop and big screen.
  • Deliberate referencing. Relevant use of titles, headings and meta descriptions for each page.
  • Prominent clickable zones. Buttons are apparent. Hyperlinks are long enough to click even on the smallest screens.
  • Image identification. All pictures have a description in the HTML code (the alt attribute). Almost every photograph has a phrase to complement the main content.
  • Safe contrast. There're dark texts against pale backgrounds. I also tested readability for colorblind users.

Simulation of colour blindness. In this case, I was testing protanomaly (reduced sensitivity to red light).

Content

  • Good readability. Overall, the readable level is five. I've avoided the use of passive voice, adverbs and jargon without explanation.
  • Scannable text. Short phrases, short paragraphs. One idea at a time and the first one is often a summary. Titles give a fair idea of the content.
  • Regular text sizing. Characters' size is at least 16 pixels, and it adapts to the viewport.
  • Lightness. I optimized all images to reduce the time required to download the website. Also, I disregarded content management systems to keep a cleaner code.
  • Available further content. I'm aware that this case study is longer than the wintertime in Canada. For this reason, I hid and labelled the details in an accordion. If most users ignore them, it's not a concern as the main content sums it all. That eases scrolling down while allowing others to get more details if needed.
  • Reliability. There are some quotes and hyperlinks to the sources. I incorporated them not only to back my ideas but to acknowledge the wise people that have taught me so much.
  • Limited use of instructions. I tried to make all the content self-explanatory. Still, I added some guidance to help the users in a few introductory texts.
“In Don't Make Me Think, Steve Krug boldly claims that instructions must die. Do yourself a favour and ignore such advice. Removing good instructions requires more thought from users.” —Everett N. McKay
